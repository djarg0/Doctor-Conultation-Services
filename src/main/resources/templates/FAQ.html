Dental Care

  • What type of toothbrush and toothpaste should I use?

    It’s best to use a soft-bristled toothbrush, as it effectively cleans your teeth without damaging your gums. Choose a toothbrush that fits comfortably in your mouth and allows you to reach all areas easily. For toothpaste, use one that contains fluoride—it helps strengthen enamel and prevent cavities. If you have specific dental concerns, your dentist may recommend a specialized toothpaste.

  • Do I really need to floss?

    Yes, flossing is essential. It removes plaque and food particles from between your teeth where your toothbrush can’t reach. Regular flossing helps prevent cavities, gum disease, and bad breath. Brushing alone isn’t enough for complete oral hygiene.

  • How do fillings work?

    Fillings repair cavities caused by tooth decay. During the procedure, the dentist removes the decayed part of the tooth and fills the space with a durable material like composite resin, amalgam, or ceramic. This restores the tooth’s shape and function, and helps prevent further decay.

Cardiology

  • What is Cardiologist?

    A cardiologist is a doctor who specializes in diagnosing and treating conditions related to the heart and blood vessels. They help manage heart diseases, high blood pressure, heart attacks, and other cardiovascular issues. Cardiologists also guide patients on heart-healthy lifestyles and preventive care.

  • How does a stress test work?

    A stress test measures how your heart performs under physical activity. You’ll usually walk on a treadmill or ride a stationary bike while your heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing are monitored. It helps doctors detect heart problems that might not show up when you're at rest, such as blocked arteries or irregular heart rhythms.

  • Do cardiac tests hurt?

    Most cardiac tests are painless or cause only minimal discomfort. Tests like EKGs, echocardiograms, and stress tests are non-invasive and generally comfortable. Some tests, like angiograms, may involve minor discomfort due to injections or catheter insertion, but your doctor will explain what to expect and help keep you comfortable throughout.

Diagnosis

  • What do we know about diagnostic error?

    Diagnostic error occurs when a health condition is missed, delayed, or incorrectly diagnosed. It can happen due to complex symptoms, communication gaps, or limitations in tests. While not always preventable, improving doctor-patient communication and seeking second opinions can help reduce the risk of errors. Early detection and accurate diagnosis are key to effective treatment.

  • How often does it happen?

    Diagnostic errors are estimated to affect about 10-15% of patients at some point in their healthcare journey. While many are minor, some can lead to serious consequences. Healthcare providers continuously work to improve diagnostic accuracy through better training, technology, and communication.

  • What are the obstacles to accurate diagnosis?

    Obstacles include complex or unclear symptoms, limited patient information, time constraints during consultations, and communication gaps between patients and doctors. Additionally, limitations of diagnostic tests and human error can also affect accuracy. Overcoming these challenges requires thorough evaluation, good communication, and sometimes additional testing.
